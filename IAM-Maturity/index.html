IAM Maturity Assessment

What is Identity and Access Management (IAM)?

Identity and Access Management (IAM) is the foundation of digital trust in an organization. It is the framework of policies, processes, and technologies used to manage digital identities and control who can access what, when, and how. It ensures the right people have the right access at the right time, enforces security policies, and prevents unauthorized access, protecting critical information assets.
IAM brings together three critical capabilities:
    Identity Governance & Administration (IGA): Managing the lifecycle of identities and access — from onboarding (Joiner), role changes (Mover), to exit (Leaver). This includes access requests, approvals, certifications, and compliance reporting. Access Management: Controlling authentication and authorization to applications, systems, APIs, and data — including Single Sign-On (SSO), Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA), and federation. AAA Principles (Authentication, Authorization, Accounting): Verifying identity, enforcing access decisions, and recording access activity for security, audit, and investigation.
    Together, IAM ensures that the right people have the right access at the right time, for the right reasons, while protecting applications, data, brand reputation, and customer trust.

What is IAM Maturity & Why it Matters?

IAM Maturity measures how effectively an organization manages identities and access. Higher maturity means consistent, predictable, and risk-aware IAM processes. Assessing maturity helps organizations to:

  • Identify gaps and risks in identity management
  • Plan improvements and automation
  • Align IAM with industry frameworks (NIST, CRI, ISO)
  • Support audits and compliance efforts

How IAM Maturity is Calculated

Maturity is calculated by:

  1. Defining IAM domains (areas of IAM capability)
    2. The first step in measuring IAM maturity is to identify the key functional areas — or domains — that represent the organization’s IAM capabilities.   2. Assessing each domain through specific questions
    3. Once IAM domains are defined, the next step is to evaluate how mature each domain is by asking targeted questions. Each question focuses on a specific capability, process, or control within the domain.
  3. Scoring each question from 1 to 5
    4. After asking targeted questions for each IAM domain, each question is scored on a 1 to 5 scale to quantify maturity. The scoring reflects how well the organization performs in that specific area
  4. Calculating the average score per domain
    5. Once each question in a domain has been scored, the scores are combined to calculate a domain-level average. This provides a single numeric value representing the organization’s maturity in that domain. For example, if a domain has three questions scored 2, 3, and 4, the domain score is calculated as: (2 + 3 + 4) / 3 = 3.0
  5. Calculating overall IAM maturity by averaging domain scores
    6. After calculating scores for each domain, the overall IAM maturity score is determined by averaging all domain scores. This single number provides a holistic view of the organization’s IAM capability.
  6. Mapping the score to a maturity level (Level 1–5)
    7. To make the assessment results understandable and actionable, the numeric score is mapped to a descriptive maturity level.
